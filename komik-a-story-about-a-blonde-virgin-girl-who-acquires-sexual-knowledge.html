39. Make a story about you??


Astraphobia (fear of thunder & lightning) — sad

My cheeks were streaked with tears as another lightning struck. I was waiting for the thunder to follow as I hugged myself into the corner of my bed. For most people, they might fear the sight of lightning and the sound of thunder but, I, I was reminded of a sorrowful memory. Imagine having to go through all the tears and tissues everytime it rains. Having to relive the painful memory of losing those you love. All at once, gone. They were gone.

It was the fall of October 1999, the kitchen was warm and homely with the inviting smell of pumpkin pie. My mother and I were busy in the kitchen. Sweat dripped from my forehead as I waited beside the oven. It was almost 6, dinner should be served soon. The calendar beside the ticking wall clock showed the date, the 31st. It was that spooky time of the year, Halloween. The house was covered in black and orange decorations, and the scent of flavourful candy was all around our home. I still remember every single detail of that night. The night of my most tragic nightmare and the memory was as fresh as a daisy in my head.

My little brother, who was 8, went out trick-or-treating around the neighbourhood with my father. He looked so small in his over-sized jet black jumper, with a full skeleton pattern printed on it, knocking on every door while trying to act scary and menacing. He even runs, walks, and dances as if he was an actual skeleton, such a precious little being. Everytime he says ‘Boo!’ to his neighbours, the smile on our father’s face grew wider, he just brings that kind of joy around. The day was perfectly calm and lovely, in fact, it was wonderful.

However, the peace was soon washed away when a heavy dread settled at the bottom of my heart. The wall clock struck 7, they should be home by now. After all, our neighbourhood wasn’t that big, so an hour should be enough to knock on every single door, and say the usual phrase ‘Trick or treat?”. I realised I was biting my already-short nails, a negative habit I developed as a result of stress.

The rain started to fall, soft and light at first, but then grew louder till the raindrops were pounding on the concrete pavement outside. I was worried sick when I suddenly saw them running through the playground, across our house. Although the rain was heavy and it was starting to fog up the window, I could see that their faces were plastered with a smile, and I couldn’t help but smile back.

I dashed to the front door when I heard the sound of thunder. As I opened the door, I yelled ‘Come in!!’ to the both of them. They were making their way through the swing sets when my little brother stumbled onto the sand. As he tried to get up, it happened, lightning struck them, it was so bright, my eyes started to burn. My head was suddenly spinning and my vision was blurry. The loud sound had also deafen my ears, they were buzzing. Then, I realised that all I could do was scream, and so I did. I screamed till my voice was sore, and I felt my mother’s comforting hug, trying desperately to calm me. The rest of the incident was quite blurry, as my vision was temporarily gone.

The next morning, I opened my eyes and stared at a ceiling, different from the one in my room. I sat up too quickly and felt a jab of pain shooting down my spine. My back was aching, and so was my head. I looked around and realised that I was in a hospital bed. The thick curtains were draped infront of the tall, wide windows, blocking out most of the sunlight. This part of my memory was also quite blurry since I was only around the age of 12 then.

I could only recall the moment when my mother and I were told that my father and brother were gone. Their bodies were burnt, as black as the skies of starless nights, crisp, some parts into ashes. As I heard, my eyes were filled with tears, and I couldn’t help but let it all out. They were both gone from my world, my own family. I couldn’t bear it. My pathetic screams of rage and devastation echoed the hospital hallways, I was out of my mind. My mother helplessly cried, while the doctor desperately tried to calm us.

Till this day, the memory has stuck with me, while lightnings and thunders reminded me of the incident, thus making my astraphobia worse. I try, I really do try, to overcome my fear. I’ve been to countless therapy sessions with many different therapists, none of them help me for long. Most nights, I sleep under my covers, afraid that it might rain too heavily, causing lightning to struck. I do not want to cry anymore. I long to be helped.

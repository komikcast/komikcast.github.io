37. adding lines to form right angled triangles that wheren't originally there​


Picture Nr. 1.
Area = √3 square units.

Picture Nr. 2.
x = 11
 

Penjelasan dengan langkah-langkah:

Picture Nr. 1

That triangle is an equilateral triangle. So, we add a line from a vertex, and that line must be perpendicular to the side in front of that vertex. So, we get 2 right triangles, each of which has base [tex]b = 1[/tex] and hypotenuse [tex]c = 2[/tex]. Let [tex]a[/tex] be the height of the triangle.

The area of the triangle shown in picture nr. 1 is:

[tex]\begin{aligned}A&=\cancel{2}\cdot\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\cdot b\cdot a\\&=b\cdot\sqrt{c^2-b^2}\quad\textsf{by Pythagorean theorem}\\&=1\cdot\sqrt{2^2-1^2}\\&=\sqrt{4-1}\\\therefore\ A&=\boxed{\bf\sqrt{3}\ \sf square\ units.}\end{aligned}[/tex]
_________________

Picture Nr. 2

Please see the attached picture. We add a line connecting 2 vertices, so we can get 2 right triangles.

Let [tex]y[/tex] be the length of that line.

By Pythagorean theorem:
[tex]\begin{aligned}x&=\sqrt{y^2-3^2}=\sqrt{y^2-9}\end{aligned}[/tex]

Again, by Pythagorean theorem:
[tex]\begin{aligned}y^2=9^2+7^2=81+49=130\end{aligned}[/tex]

Thus:

[tex]\begin{aligned}x&=\sqrt{130-9}=\sqrt{121}\\\therefore\ x&=\boxed{\bf11}\end{aligned}[/tex]
